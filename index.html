Har Ghar Jal —
Every Household, A Tap

A structured teardown of the Jal Jeevan Mission: who it moves, how it targets and pays, and where the gap between a connection and a functioning tap is quietly widening.

Scheme Jal Jeevan Mission (JJM) Launched 15 Aug 2019 Current phase JJM 2.0, approved Mar 2026 Target Tap water to every rural household by 2028

Assignment brief. Har Ghar Jal ("water to every household") is the rural drinking-water initiative of the Jal Jeevan Mission, run by the central government to give every rural household a Functional Household Tap Connection (FHTC) delivering 55 litres per person per day. This assignment maps the scheme across five lenses — stakeholders, targeting and delivery, financing, outcomes, and societal reaction — and argues that the scheme's central tension is coverage versus functionality: a household can be "connected" on paper without water actually reaching the tap.

The number that matters

15.80 crore households now have a tap — but not all of them have water.

Coverage has climbed from 17% (3.23 crore households) in 2019 to roughly 81.6% by mid-2026. A 2024 government-commissioned survey found that while nearly 98% of rural households have a physical connection, only about three-fourths receive regular, safe, adequate supply. That last gap is the real target.

Stakeholders & Incentives

Ministers & Departments

  • Jal Shakti Ministry — owns credit for the flagship scheme
  • Finance Ministry — carries the fiscal burden of the ₹8.69 lakh crore outlay
  • Chief Ministers / state governments — political win, sharpened in states on a 50:50 cost split
  • PHED / PWD / state water boards — implementation on the ground
  • Panchayati Raj & Rural Development — coordination layer

Frontline & Beneficiaries

  • Village Water & Sanitation Committees (VWSC) — operations & maintenance, tariff collection, mandated 50% women members
  • Plumbers & contractors — installation
  • Women's SHGs — water-quality testing via Field Test Kits
  • Rural households, women & girls, children — time saved, health, drudgery reduction, school/anganwadi access
Watch Households in water-scarce or arsenic/fluoride-contaminated regions, remote and tribal hamlets, and villages that are "certified" but not actually flowing sit outside the incentive structure that rewards certification over service.
02

Targeting & Delivery Mechanism

  • Eligibility — universal, no income or category test; every rural household in Gram Panchayat areas. Urban India is excluded, covered instead under AMRUT 2.0.
  • Registration — application through the Gram Panchayat or VWSC, tracked with an application ID on the jaljeevanmission.gov.in portal.
  • Delivery mix — piped tap water only, never cash or vouchers. Cost-sharing runs 100:0 (Centre:UT), 90:10 (North-East/Himalayan states), and 50:50 for other states.
  • Verification — Field Test Kits for local water-quality checks, plus "Har Ghar Jal" village certification. But certification is infrastructure-based, and the 2024 functionality survey found it doesn't reliably predict actual supply.
Design question Should certification require a functionality audit — water actually flowing weekly — rather than just confirming a tap is installed?
03

Financing & Sustainability

The money

  • Total outlay: ₹8.69 lakh crore under JJM 2.0
  • Central share: ₹3.59 lakh crore, states covering the balance per the sharing ratio
  • Recent disbursement: ₹1,561 crore released in April 2026 to five states, including ₹792.93 crore to Uttar Pradesh

Built-in sustainability

  • Mandatory grey-water management via soak pits
  • Rainwater harvesting and groundwater recharge designed in from the start
  • Structural pivot in JJM 2.0: from an infrastructure-centric model to utility-based service delivery — paying for reliability, not just pipe-laying
Open question Is there a tariff or user-fee model funding long-term O&M, or does the scheme stay centrally subsidised indefinitely?

State variance is wide: 11 states and Union Territories — including Goa, Telangana, Haryana, Punjab, Gujarat, Himachal Pradesh, and Puducherry — have reached 100% coverage, while 8 states remain below 80%. Beyond the headline numbers, the assignment should track: reduction in waterborne disease and time saved (translating into school attendance and women's labour-force participation); arsenic and fluoride levels from community testing; and whether excluded or remote groups are closing the gap, not just national averages.

Societal Reactions & Impacts to Anticipate

Political & governance friction

  • Opposition-ruled states may contest funding allocation or credit-claiming
  • The scheme is tied to the PM's 2019 Independence Day announcement, keeping it politically salient
  • VWSCs often lack the technical capacity to fix source or pump failures RTI filings are already a common citizen recourse when a tap is installed but dry

Risk & scrutiny

  • Fraud/leakage risk from inflated "certified village" counts that don't reflect real functionality
  • The gap between official coverage (~98%) and functionality survey data (~75%) is likely to draw "installation over service" criticism
  • Unintended consequence to watch: over-extraction of groundwater in water-stressed regions if recharge isn't implemented alongside new connections

Throughline

Design promised a connection. Delivery has to promise water.

Every lens above lands on the same fault line: JJM was built and funded to lay pipe, and it has done that at genuine scale — from 17% to 82% coverage in six years. But a scheme measured by taps installed will always outrun a scheme measured by water delivered, unless the incentive structure — certification, financing, and O&M — is rebuilt around functionality rather than infrastructure.

For the mind map: put this gap, not the coverage number, at the centre. It's the "pilot and iterate" note the assignment is asking for — JJM 2.0's shift toward "utility-based service delivery" is the government's own acknowledgment that the first version optimised the wrong metric.
